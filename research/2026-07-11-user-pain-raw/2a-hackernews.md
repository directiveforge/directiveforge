## What surprised me
The single highest-signal thread in the entire beat (749 points, 638 comments — by far the largest of anything I found on this topic) is not about an agent writing bad code. It's a developer who got their Anthropic account banned with no explanation after running multiple Claude Code instances that iterated on each other's CLAUDE.md file. The top comments aren't about workflow quality at all — they're about vendor trust, opaque moderation, and "you'll never hear back from Anthropic." That's a category of pain no rules/skills generator touches, and it's the loudest thing on HN right now adjacent to our exact keyword.

Closely related and unexpected: one credible comment theory (verdverm, 46725764) is that the ban happened because *iterating on claude.md files itself pattern-matches to prompt-injection behavior* to Anthropic's safety systems, especially with multiple agent instances editing each other's context. A product that actively encourages generating and iterating on CLAUDE.md/rules/skills files at scale should be aware this is a live platform-level risk, not just a quality risk.

Second surprise: the highest-engagement "how to write CLAUDE.md" thread (748 points, 290 comments) is dominated at the top not by tips but by people rejecting the entire premise — "The only good Claude.md is a deleted Claude.md," "'Here's how to use the slop machine better' is such a ridiculous pretense," "this whole CLAUDE.md thing seems a temporary kludge." The genre DirectiveForge automates (writing/tuning CLAUDE.md) is itself an object of ridicule in its own highest-traffic discussion thread.

Third: Superpowers (obra/Jesse Vincent) and spec-kit both have real, articulate detractors saying the framework made output *worse*, not better ("Claude makes more mistakes when using superpowers than when not") or that it's process theater ("waterfall, but with AI, now?"). These aren't fringe takes — they sit right under otherwise-positive threads. Any generator that scaffolds a skills/rules layer should expect this exact objection on Show HN.

Fourth: token/usage-limit anxiety has become its own named phenomenon ("token anxiety") completely decoupled from workflow-file quality — it's about opaque billing and unpredictable consumption (one person burned 12% of a session budget typing "test one two three"). This is a distinct pain lane a knowledge-base/rules generator has zero leverage over, but it's clearly top-of-mind for the same Show HN audience.

Fifth: a top comment on the token-efficiency thread (monooso, citing OpenRouter data) points out that ~93% of coding-agent token spend is input, not output — meaning most "shrink your CLAUDE.md to save tokens" advice targets the wrong 4-7% of the cost. If DirectiveForge's harness or pitch leans on token savings, this is exactly the kind of rebuttal to expect, and it argues for measuring context/input efficiency, not output verbosity.

Sixth: I expected "review burden of AI-written code" to show up mostly as an individual-developer complaint. Instead the sharpest version of it is a maintainer problem — Godot and other OSS projects "drowning in AI slop pull requests" from contributors who submitted AI-generated code they don't understand themselves. That's a team/community-boundary pain, structurally different from "my personal CLAUDE.md isn't good enough."

Seventh: there's a consistent "blame-the-user" backlash meme across at least three separate threads (sandrello, Uptrenda, candiddevmike) — the complaint that when the base agent underperforms, the industry response is "you just needed better markdown/plugins/skills," and commenters read that as a tell that the underlying product doesn't work, rather than as legitimate tooling advice. Any generator whose value prop is "we configure it correctly for you" needs to actively differentiate from this pattern, not just do it better.

Eighth: a top comment tears apart a token-efficiency *benchmark* specifically because it only measured output length with no accuracy check ("I could obliterate this benchmark with the prompt 'Always answer with one word'"). Given DirectiveForge's pitch explicitly leans on a "measured-outcomes harness," this is close to a dry run of the exact adversarial response Show HN commenters will produce against any benchmark claim that isn't airtight on methodology.

Ninth: the "context rot" Ask HN thread skewed toward general engineering-documentation decay (ER diagrams nobody maintains, tribal knowledge that leaves when senior engineers leave) more than agent-specific rot. This suggests the pain is broader than "my CLAUDE.md is stale" — it's "all forms of system documentation rot," and a workflow-kit pitch framed narrowly around agent files may undersell (or oversell) what it actually fixes.

Tenth: despite hunting specifically for SuperClaude, BMAD-METHOD, and claude-flow launch-thread backlash, none of them have any real HN traction (all under 10 points across every hit) — the actual locus of rival-tool skepticism on HN right now is Superpowers, spec-kit, and CLAUDE.md-authoring posts, not the multi-agent-orchestration frameworks I expected to be the loudest rivals.
